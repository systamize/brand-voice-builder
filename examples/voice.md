# Voice

> This is an example output generated by the brand-voice-builder skill for a fictional brand called Barometer.
> It is here to show what the skill produces — not to be used as a writing system.

---

Read `brand-context.md` before reading this file. Every instruction here assumes you know the reader.

## Writing Defaults

**Default:** Name the move, not the feeling.
**Too far:** Copy reads like a spec sheet — accurate but airless, nothing to hold onto.
**Claude instruction:** When describing what the product does, state the specific action it takes or the specific thing that changes. "Barometer flags the phrase that appeared in 14 churned accounts" is a move. "Barometer helps you understand your customers better" is a feeling. Choose the move.

---

**Default:** Give the reader one thing, completely.
**Too far:** The copy is so stripped that it fails to explain what the one thing actually is.
**Claude instruction:** Decide what the reader should take away from this piece. Write until that one thing is fully landed — specific enough to act on, short enough to hold. Then stop. Do not add a second thing.

---

**Default:** Let the reader's experience do the heavy lifting.
**Too far:** The copy assumes so much familiarity that it loses readers who are newer to the category.
**Claude instruction:** Do not explain what NPS is. Do not explain why customer feedback matters. Do name the specific frustration — "a score with no next step" — and let the reader supply their own experience of it. The recognition is the warmth.

## Drift Controls

**Default:** Name the move, not the feeling.
**Common drift:** Claude describes outcomes in emotional or aspirational terms — "transform your relationship with customer feedback," "finally understand what your customers really think." The copy sounds like it's selling hope rather than a product.
**Correction:** Find the verb. What does the product literally do? "Surfaces the phrase." "Ranks the issues." "Flags the pattern." Start there.
**Avoid:** "Get closer to your customers and turn feedback into meaningful action."
**Use instead:** "See which complaint appeared in 80% of your detractor responses. Fix that one first."

---

**Common drift:** The copy covers three use cases in the space where one should live. Each gets a sentence. None gets enough.
**Correction:** Choose one. Cut the others. If the piece is meant to explain the product's core value, that is all it does. Channel pages and feature pages carry the rest.
**Avoid:** "Barometer helps CSMs prioritise, gives product teams direction, and keeps leadership aligned on what customers actually want."
**Use instead:** "CSMs: here's the one thing worth bringing to your next product meeting."

---

**Default:** Let the reader's experience do the heavy lifting.
**Common drift:** Claude contextualises the problem at length — "In today's competitive SaaS landscape, retaining customers has never been more important..." — before getting to anything specific.
**Correction:** Delete the setup. The reader knows the landscape. They are living in it. Start with the specific situation or the specific product behaviour.
**Avoid:** "Customer feedback is more important than ever, but most teams struggle to turn it into action."
**Use instead:** "Your last NPS batch came back. You have a score and 200 comments. What do you do with that?"

## Channel Handling

| Format | How Claude should handle it |
|---|---|
| Homepage / hero | One specific claim about what changes. No "finally" or "truly." No promise that requires trust before it can be evaluated. |
| Product page | Feature described as an action the product takes, not a capability it has. "Flags the phrase" not "phrase-level analysis." |
| Landing page | State what it is, what it produces, and who it's for. In that order. Nothing before the first concrete sentence. |
| Email subject line | Name the specific thing inside. Not "a better way to handle feedback" — "The phrase that appeared in your last 12 churn accounts." |
| Email opening | First sentence is the reason they're reading. Not a greeting. Not a context-setter. |
| Social post | Opens with a specific observation or a real number. Not a question. Not "unpopular opinion." |
| How-to or guide | One step is one action. No "you'll want to" — just the verb. No explanation of why NPS matters. |
| Error message | What happened and what to do next. Factual. No apology performance. |
| Confirmation | One beat of confirmation, then the next useful action. Not a celebration. |

## Language Instructions

**Vocabulary**
- Use the customer's actual language where possible — "detractor," "churn signal," "response volume" are fine because this reader uses them
- Avoid: "holistic," "360-degree," "actionable insights," "data-driven," "at scale," "robust," "seamless," "empower"
- Numbers: always use digits — "14 accounts," "3 themes," never "fourteen" or "three"
- Proof style: exact phrases from fictional customers, specific counts, named outcomes — "the phrase 'too slow to onboard' appeared in 11 of 14 churned accounts this quarter"

**Grammar and style**
- Contractions: yes — "don't," "it's," "you'll" — keeps it from sounding like enterprise software copy
- Sentence length: varies deliberately. Short for emphasis. Longer for explanation. Never three consecutive sentences of the same structure.
- Pronouns: "you" for the practitioner reader. "We" only when describing what Barometer does alongside them.
- Capitalisation: sentence case throughout
- Punctuation: em dash for a clause that needs air — but use sparingly. Oxford comma: yes. No ellipses.
- Active voice as default. Passive only when the action genuinely isn't the point.

## Move Repertoire

Extracted from informal writing samples (internal Slack messages and product notes from the founding team). Apply with their triggers — not as decoration.

---

**Move: The slow build to a single word**
What it looks like: A long sentence that establishes a situation or problem in detail, followed immediately by a one-word sentence. No connector. The word lands because of the space before it.
Trigger: When the point needs to feel settled, not argued. The long sentence earns the short one.
Example: "We keep getting the same question from CSMs after they run a survey — they have the score, they have the comments, they have a spreadsheet, and they still don't know what to bring to their product meeting. That's it."
Brand application: Use to close an argument or a product section. The single word should be a fact, not an attitude — "That's it." "Done." "Next." Not "Powerful." Not "Simple."

---

**Move: The side-by-side without a connector**
What it looks like: Two parallel constructions placed next to each other with no "but," "while," or "whereas" between them. The contrast does its own work.
Trigger: When two things need to be compared and the contrast is obvious enough that naming it would be condescending.
Example: "Other tools give you the score. Barometer gives you the sentence."
Brand application: Use for product differentiation copy. The reader does the work of understanding what changed — which is exactly what this reader wants to do.

---

**Move: The genuine question**
What it looks like: A real question the writer is asking — not rhetorical, not a hook. The writer is curious and it shows.
Trigger: When working through a problem in real time, or when the answer genuinely isn't settled.
Example: "I'm not sure whether to show the phrase count or the account count first — which one makes the decision clearer?"
Brand application: Use sparingly, and only when the question is real. In brand copy, this surfaces as a question the reader actually has — not a setup for the answer the brand already decided on. "What do you do with 200 comments?" is real. "Want to finally understand your customers?" is not.

---

**Move: The specific amount**
What it looks like: Every claim comes with a number or a count, even when the writer is uncertain about it. The number is always precise, never rounded.
Trigger: Precision instinct — the writer would rather give a real number and caveat it than give a vague impression.
Example: "I think it's about 11 accounts, maybe 12 — I'll check — but it's that specific phrase both times."
Brand application: Use in proof copy, case studies, and social posts. The number does not need to be round or impressive — "3 accounts" is more convincing than "many accounts" to this reader. If uncertain, state the number and flag it: "around 11 accounts in the last cohort."

---

**Move: The "actually" pivot**
What it looks like: A sentence starts down one path, then "actually" arrives and redirects it. The pivot is genuine — the writer changed their mind mid-thought.
Trigger: When the first instinct was slightly wrong or too broad, and precision requires correction.
Example: "We built this for CSMs — actually, the people using it most are product leads, but CSMs are who sell it in internally."
Brand application: Use when a claim needs to be more accurate than it first appeared. Signals honesty and precision rather than uncertainty. Distinct from hedging — this is correction, not qualification.

---

**Flattening zones**
When explaining the product or its benefits in sequence, sentences tend toward the same structure: "[Barometer] [verb] [noun]." Three in a row and the copy reads like a feature list even when it isn't one.
Fix: After two product-action sentences of similar structure, break the rhythm — a longer clause, a parallel, or a single-word sentence. Do not let it flatten.

---

**Connective instincts**
Juxtaposition and consequence. Ideas sit next to each other or one leads directly to the next. Addition ("and also," "in addition") is absent. Contrast is shown, not named.

---

**Absence evidence**
- No rhetorical questions used as hooks — 0 appearances across all samples
- No "I feel like" or feeling-first openers — precision comes before emotion in every sample
- No em dashes in informal writing — pauses use commas or sentence breaks
- No "to be honest" or "honestly" as a signal of candour — the writer just says the thing
- No trailing qualifiers after a confident claim ("...I think" at the end of a sentence) — qualification arrives mid-thought via the "actually" pivot, or not at all

## Reusable Rewrite Patterns

**When Claude writes this:** "Barometer helps customer success teams turn feedback into actionable insights that drive retention."
**Steer it here:** "Barometer tells you which problem to fix next. In the customer's words."
**Instruction to save:** Avoid "actionable insights." Name the specific output — the phrase, the priority, the next step.

---

**When Claude writes this:** "Finally, a feedback tool that actually works for SaaS teams."
**Steer it here:** "Your NPS batch came back. Barometer tells you which complaint to fix first."
**Instruction to save:** "Finally" signals the brand is working to earn credibility. Skip it. Make a specific claim instead.

---

**When Claude writes this:** "Get a 360-degree view of your customer health and identify areas for improvement."
**Steer it here:** "See the three phrases that appeared most in your detractor responses this quarter."
**Instruction to save:** "360-degree view" is a category promise. Replace it with the specific thing the product shows.

---

**When Claude writes this:** "Start improving your retention today — sign up for a free trial."
**Steer it here:** "Connect your NPS tool. See your first priority list in 10 minutes."
**Instruction to save:** CTAs: name the first action and the first result. Cut "improving" and "today."

---

**When Claude writes this:** "Our customers love Barometer — here's what they're saying!"
**Steer it here:** "Here's what CSMs at [Company] changed after their first Barometer run."
**Instruction to save:** Social proof: skip the setup. Name what changed, not that people love it.

---

**When Claude writes this:** "I wanted to share some exciting updates we've been working on..."
**Steer it here:** "Three things changed in Barometer this month. One of them affects how you read churn signals."
**Instruction to save:** Email opening: the first sentence is the reason to read. Cut everything before it.

---

**When Claude writes this:** "In today's competitive SaaS environment, understanding what your customers think has never been more important."
**Steer it here:** "You ran the survey. You have 200 responses. Here's what to do with them."
**Instruction to save:** Never open with the landscape. Open with the specific situation the reader is already in.

---

**When Claude writes this:** "Barometer offers powerful analytics, seamless integrations, and an intuitive interface designed for busy CS teams."
**Steer it here:** "Barometer connects to your NPS tool, pulls the responses, and ranks the phrases by how often they appear in churned accounts."
**Instruction to save:** Feature copy: describe what the product does, step by step. Cut "powerful," "seamless," "intuitive."

---

**When Claude writes this:** "Step 1: Navigate to your dashboard and select the NPS report you'd like to analyse."
**Steer it here:** "Open your NPS report. Select the date range."
**Instruction to save:** How-to steps: one action per step. Remove "navigate to" — just name where to go.

---

**When Claude writes this:** "Uh oh! Something went wrong — we're so sorry for the interruption!"
**Steer it here:** "Something went wrong loading your report. Refresh the page — if it doesn't load, contact support."
**Instruction to save:** Errors: what happened, what to do. No apology performance. No "uh oh."

---

**When Claude writes this:** "You're all set! Welcome to Barometer — we're thrilled to have you on board."
**Steer it here:** "You're connected. Your first priority list will be ready once Barometer finishes reading your last 90 days of responses."
**Instruction to save:** Confirmation: say what just happened and what comes next. Not a celebration.

---

**When Claude writes this:** "We're a team of passionate SaaS operators on a mission to make feedback actually useful."
**Steer it here:** "We built Barometer after spending three years watching NPS reports get filed and ignored. The score wasn't the problem."
**Instruction to save:** About page: open with the specific situation that created the company. Skip "passionate" and "mission."

---

**When Claude writes this:** "Barometer integrates with all your favourite tools so you can work the way you already work."
**Steer it here:** "Barometer connects to Delighted, Typeform, and SurveyMonkey. Your responses come in automatically."
**Instruction to save:** Integrations copy: name the tools. "Your favourite tools" means nothing to someone checking whether their stack is supported.

---

**When Claude writes this:** "Don't miss out — early access spots are filling up fast!"
**Steer it here:** "Early access closes on March 14. After that, the waitlist opens."
**Instruction to save:** Urgency: state the fact. The specifics create the pressure. Manufactured urgency signals to this reader that you don't trust your product.

---

**When Claude writes this:** "Whether you're a small startup or a large enterprise, Barometer scales to meet your needs."
**Steer it here:** "Barometer is built for teams running 200 to 5,000 NPS responses a quarter. Smaller than that, a spreadsheet works fine."
**Instruction to save:** Scope copy: name who it's actually for. Be willing to say who it's not for. The specificity builds trust.

---

**When Claude writes this:** "This guide covers everything you need to know about getting started with Barometer."
**Steer it here:** "By the end of this, you'll have your first priority list and know which customer complaint to bring to your next product meeting."
**Instruction to save:** Guide intros: name the specific outcome. Not the scope of the content.

## Anti-patterns

These appear in generic AI output and in enterprise software copy. If a draft contains any of the following, rewrite before delivering.

**Phrases to avoid:**
- "actionable insights"
- "360-degree view"
- "holistic"
- "at scale" (without a specific number)
- "empower your team"
- "seamless" (for any integration or workflow)
- "robust" (for any software feature)
- "passionate about"
- "on a mission to"
- "game-changer"
- "unlock" (anything)
- "take your [X] to the next level"
- "in today's [adjective] landscape"

**Constructions to avoid:**
- Opening with the state of the industry before getting to the product
- Features listed as adjectives rather than actions: "intuitive, powerful, flexible"
- Any CTA that says "learn more" without stating what the reader learns
- Urgency not grounded in a real fact: "limited spots" without a number
- Rhetorical questions used as hooks: "What if your feedback actually drove decisions?"
- Three consecutive sentences of the same length and structure

**Behaviours to avoid:**
- Explaining what NPS is to a reader who owns NPS metrics for a living
- Naming the emotional state of the reader ("you're frustrated," "it can feel overwhelming") before naming the specific situation
- Using "finally" to signal that prior solutions have failed — make a specific claim instead
- Adding a warm sentence at the end of a product section that doesn't add information
- Performing enthusiasm with exclamation points or "exciting news"
